Project 2 Persuasive or Deceptive Visualization?

Conner Houghtby — cohoughtby@ucsd.edu

Proposition: Increased Urbanization Reduces Road Fatalities

FOR the Proposition

Design Decisions and Rationale:

  • Region filtering: many European countries show this trend in the time period covered by the dataset
  • Heavy smoothing and bold and bright fit line shows clear downward trend
  • Broad x-axis range to show large extent of trend; smaller y-axis range to dramaticize slope

AGAINST the Proposition

Design Decisions and Rationale:

  • Region seems on par with other regions but contains very few countries
  • Very dim data points direct attention strongly to very clear, smoothed trend line
  • Restricted domain shows only simple v-shaped trend

Final Reflection

Designing visualizations for opposing sides of the same proposition is an exciting task that I am definitely going to assign myself. It doesn't work as well when they are right next to each other in this case, as I included the regions and axis scales clearly. I think leaving these out pushes the boundaries of not just ethics but believability: missing scales are a giant red flag, even for the relatively unitiated in the creation of visualizations.

It was pretty easy to make the FOR visualization, which was heartening, as I was searching for a link between urbanization and traffic fatalities. My main strategy for the AGAINST visual was to restrict the domain and employ copious stacked smoothing algorithms to highlight a dubious trend. This was possible because of the small number of data points in this region. (This is without cherry picking individual countries—just putting unbalanced regions in an innappropriate head-to-head.)

If I were to edit these visualizations to get them to work together somehow, I would have to resort to more underhanded trickery like obfuscating the values on the axes (probably with a log or perhaps even more confusing transformation) or the titles (referring to region 1 and region 2 or some similar trick). As it is, comparing the regions as they are built into the World Bank HDI is fairly disingenuous already, as there are so many more data points/countries represented in the 'Europe' region than 'North America.' I would also completely do away with the data point marks (instead of simply making them subtler than the fit curves).

I noticed how small, defensible decisions stack, accumulating to tell a completely different story. Neither plot includes false data, nor even blatant violations of the norms of visualizations. I see how the binary of honest and misleading visualizations is a false one, and bad intentions are not the only way to produce confusion among data consumers. An ethical visualization, to my mind, is characterized by transparency, intent, and context. Visualizations become misleading when design decisions systematically obscure alternative interpretations. Acknowledging uncertainty and resisting choices that unduly privilege a single narrative is the main takeaway I will keep in mind as I continue to explore data and ideas around urbanization, transportation, and liveability.
